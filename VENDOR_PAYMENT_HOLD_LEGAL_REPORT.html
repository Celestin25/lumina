Vendor Payment Hold Policies: Legal Framework, Industry Practices, and Strategic Recommendations

A Comprehensive Analysis of Payment Hold Practices for Marketplace Platforms

Prepared by: Lumina Platform Research Team
Date: February 23, 2026
Document Classification: Policy Research Report
Version: 3.0

Abstract

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of payment hold practices for marketplace vendor payments in the United States, examining legal requirements, industry benchmarks, regulatory frameworks, and strategic business considerations. Through analysis of federal and state regulations, examination of major marketplace platforms (Amazon, Uber, Airbnb, Etsy), and review of payment processor guidance (Stripe), this report establishes that while technical legal maximums exist (up to 2 years via Stripe Connect), industry practice converges on 0-7 day hold periods, with longer holds reserved for risk-based scenarios. The report concludes with evidence-based recommendations for implementing compliant, competitive payment hold policies that balance fraud protection with vendor retention.

Keywords: marketplace payments, escrow, payment holds, Regulation E, FTC Act, unclaimed property, money transmission, vendor retention

1. Introduction

Marketplace platforms face a critical operational decision: how long to hold vendor payments before disbursement. This decision intersects with multiple legal frameworks, competitive dynamics, and risk management imperatives. This report synthesizes legal requirements, industry practices, and business analysis to provide actionable guidance for marketplace payment hold policies.

1.1 Research Methodology

This report draws from:

  • Primary legal sources: Federal statutes, Code of Federal Regulations, CFPB and FTC guidance
  • Official platform documentation: Public-facing policies from Amazon, Uber, Airbnb, Etsy, Payoneer
  • Payment processor documentation: Stripe Connect official documentation
  • Industry analysis: Academic and industry research on marketplace economics
  • State regulatory sources: Money transmission and unclaimed property laws

1.2 Scope and Limitations

This report focuses on US marketplace platforms using payment processors (specifically Stripe Connect). It does not constitute legal advice; platforms should consult qualified counsel for jurisdiction-specific compliance. The analysis assumes vendors are independent contractors or businesses, not employees subject to wage payment laws.

2. Legal Framework: Federal Regulations

2.1 Federal Trade Commission Act: Unfair or Deceptive Practices

The Federal Trade Commission Act (15 U.S.C. § 45) prohibits "unfair methods of competition" and "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce" (Cornell Law School, n.d.). The FTC has broad enforcement authority, with limited exceptions for banks and certain financial institutions.

2.1.1 Unfairness Standard

A practice is considered unfair under Section 5 when it:

  1. Causes or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers
  2. The injury cannot be reasonably avoided by consumers
  3. The injury is not outweighed by countervailing benefits to consumers or competition (FTC, 1980; FTC, 2022).

For marketplace payment holds, this means:

  • Disclosure is necessary but not sufficient: Even if hold periods are disclosed, excessively long or arbitrarily applied holds may still be unfair if they cause substantial harm without adequate justification.
  • Justification matters: Hold periods must be reasonably related to legitimate business purposes (fraud prevention, chargeback protection, return windows).
  • Avoidability: Vendors must have practical means to understand and potentially appeal hold decisions.

2.1.2 Deception Standard

A practice is deceptive if it:

  • Contains a representation, omission, or practice likely to mislead reasonable consumers
  • The representation is material (affects consumer behavior or decision-making)
  • The practice is likely to cause consumer injury (FTC, 1983).

Implication: Payment hold policies must be clearly disclosed in vendor agreements and onboarding materials. Ambiguous or hidden hold policies risk FTC enforcement action.

Legal Citation: 15 U.S.C. § 45; FTC Policy Statement on Unfairness (1980); FTC Policy Statement on Deception (1983).

2.2 Consumer Financial Protection Bureau: Regulation E and Digital Payment Oversight

2.2.1 Regulation E (Electronic Fund Transfer Act)

Regulation E (12 CFR Part 1005) implements the Electronic Fund Transfer Act (EFTA), governing electronic fund transfers (EFTs) from consumer accounts (CFPB, n.d.-a). An EFT is defined as "any transfer of funds initiated through an electronic terminal, telephone, computer, or magnetic tape for the purpose of ordering, instructing, or authorizing a financial institution to debit or credit a consumer's account" (12 CFR § 1005.3).

Applicability to Marketplaces:

  • Consumer payments: When customers pay via debit card, credit-push P2P transfers, or mobile/online payments, Regulation E applies to the incoming payment side of transactions (CFPB, n.d.-a).
  • Vendor payouts: Regulation E generally does not apply to payouts to business vendors, but platforms must still handle consumer disputes and chargebacks in compliance with Reg E requirements.
  • Error resolution: Platforms must provide consumers with error resolution rights for unauthorized or incorrect transfers, which creates operational justification for holding funds to cover potential refunds.

Legal Citation: 12 CFR Part 1005; CFPB Electronic Fund Transfer FAQs (n.d.-a).

2.2.2 CFPB Supervision of Digital Payment Apps

On November 21, 2024, the CFPB issued a final rule establishing supervision over "larger participants" in the general-use digital consumer payment applications market—those facilitating at least 50 million annual USD transactions (CFPB, 2024). This rule, effective January 9, 2025, allows the CFPB to examine compliance with consumer financial protection laws, including prohibitions on unfair, deceptive, and abusive practices (UDAAP) and the Electronic Fund Transfer Act.

Implication for Marketplaces:

  • Large marketplaces may be subject to CFPB examination if they meet transaction volume thresholds.
  • Payment hold policies will be scrutinized for UDAAP compliance.
  • Platforms must ensure hold periods are justified, disclosed, and not abusive.

Legal Citation: CFPB Final Rule: Defining Larger Participants of a Market for General-Use Digital Consumer Payment Applications (2024); Federal Register Vol. 89, No. 238 (2024).

2.3 Money Transmission: FinCEN and Bank Secrecy Act

While marketplace platforms using payment processors like Stripe Connect typically do not act as money transmitters themselves (Stripe is the licensed entity), platforms must understand the regulatory framework to ensure their payment flows comply.

Key Considerations:

  • FinCEN Registration: If a platform directly receives and transmits funds (rather than using a licensed processor), it may need to register as a Money Services Business (MSB) with FinCEN.
  • State Licensing: State money transmitter licenses may be required if platforms hold funds in their own accounts rather than through a licensed processor.
  • AML/KYC Requirements: Platforms holding funds may need anti-money laundering and know-your-customer programs.

Legal Citation: Bank Secrecy Act, 31 U.S.C. § 5311 et seq.; FinCEN Money Services Business Registration.

3. Legal Framework: State Regulations

3.1 Unclaimed Property (Escheat) Laws

All 50 states have unclaimed property laws requiring businesses to remit abandoned property to state administrators after specified dormancy periods (California State Controller, n.d.). The Revised Uniform Unclaimed Property Act (RUUPA), finalized in 2016, provides a model framework adopted by many states (Uniform Law Commission, 2016).

3.1.1 Dormancy Periods

Standard Dormancy Periods:

  • General property: Typically 3-5 years of inactivity
  • Vendor credits/balances: Generally 3 years from last activity or contact
  • Uncashed checks: Often 1-3 years depending on state

Requirements:

  1. Due diligence: Holders must attempt to contact owners before reporting property as unclaimed
  2. Reporting: Annual reporting to state administrators
  3. Remittance: Delivery of property to state after dormancy period expires

Implication for Payment Holds:

  • Platforms cannot indefinitely hold vendor balances as business income
  • Dormant vendor accounts with unclaimed balances must be reported and remitted to states
  • This creates a practical outer limit on how long funds can remain in platform accounts, even if payment processor terms allow longer holds

Legal Citation: Revised Uniform Unclaimed Property Act (RUUPA), 2016; California Unclaimed Property Law; Texas Property Code Chapter 74; Maryland Unclaimed Property Holders Reporting Manual.

3.2 State Money Transmission Laws

State money transmission laws vary significantly but generally require licensing for entities that receive money for transmission to another location, hold funds in trust or escrow for others, or issue or sell payment instruments or stored value.

Key State Examples:

Massachusetts (209 CMR 44.00): Requires tangible net worth of greater of $100,000 or percentage of assets (3% for first $100M, 2% for $100M-$1B, 0.5% for over $1B); mandatory surety bonds; annual renewal via NMLS (Massachusetts Division of Banks, n.d.).

Illinois (Transmitters of Money Act): Application fee: $100 plus $10 per authorized seller location; requires FinCEN registration, AML/BSA program documentation; material changes must be reported within 15 days (Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation, n.d.).

Money Transmission Modernization Act (MTMA): Thirty-one states have enacted MTMA, establishing nationwide standards; licensed transmitters account for 99% of reported money transmission activity (CSBS, n.d.).

Implication: Platforms using licensed processors (like Stripe Connect) typically avoid direct money transmitter licensing. Platforms holding funds in their own accounts may trigger licensing requirements.

Legal Citation: CSBS Money Transmission Modernization Act; Massachusetts 209 CMR 44.00; Illinois Transmitters of Money Act; Texas Finance Code Chapter 152.

4. Industry Practices: Major Marketplace Platforms

4.1 Amazon Marketplace

Hold Period: 7 days after delivery ("DD + 7" reserve policy)
Payout Schedule: Bi-weekly (every 14 days)
Processing Time: 3-5 business days after payout initiation

Amazon implements a 7-day hold period after delivery before funds become available for disbursement (Amazon, 2025). This policy, known as "DD + 7" (delivery date + 7 days), serves multiple purposes:

  1. Return window coverage: Allows buyers time to inspect products and request refunds
  2. Chargeback protection: Ensures funds are available for chargebacks and claims
  3. Financial obligations: Maintains reserves for platform fees and obligations

Account-Level Reserves: Amazon may place an "account-level reserve" holding back all or part of payment for more than 14 days if there are account health concerns, policy violations, or high order defect rates (Amazon SP-API, 2025).

Citation: Amazon Seller Payments (2025); Amazon SP-API: Understanding Deferred Transactions (2025).

4.2 Uber

Hold Period: No explicit hold period
Payout Schedule: Weekly
Timeline: Earnings cycle ends Monday 3:59 AM → Processed Wednesday → Deposited Thursday

Uber drivers are paid on a weekly schedule via direct deposit, with no explicit hold period mentioned in official documentation (Uber Help, n.d.). Risk management is achieved through real-time fraud detection, driver rating systems, and account deactivation for policy violations.

Citation: Uber Help: Understanding Your Earnings Timeline (n.d.); Uber Help: How Payments Work (n.d.).

4.3 Airbnb

Hold Period: No explicit hold period; payouts released after check-in
Payout Schedule: Released by end of business day after guest's scheduled check-in date (or checkout for new hosts)
Processing Time: 3-5 business days for bank accounts, 3 business days for ACH, within 1 business day for PayPal

For most home stay reservations, Airbnb releases payouts by the end of the business day after the guest's scheduled check-in date (Airbnb Help, n.d.). New hosts without two completed stays receive payouts by the end of the business day after the guest's scheduled checkout date.

Special Cases: Monthly stays (28+ nights): First month's payment held for 24 hours after check-in. Fraud review may delay payouts up to 45 days after guest check-in.

Citation: Airbnb Help: When You'll Get Your Payout (n.d.); Airbnb Help: Monthly Stays Payout (n.d.).

4.4 Etsy

Hold Period: Variable reserve percentage held until tracking confirmation or default holding period expires
Payout Schedule: Weekly (default for new sellers), configurable to daily, weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly

Etsy implements a risk-based reserve system where a percentage of funds from each new physical item sale is held (Etsy Help, n.d.). Reserved funds are released: (1) immediately with valid tracking confirmation, or (2) after default holding period expires without tracking.

Citation: Etsy Help: What is a Payment Account Reserve? (n.d.); Etsy Help: How to Receive Your Etsy Payments Deposit (n.d.).

4.5 Payoneer

Hold Period: Not explicitly documented
Payout Schedule: Payments can be scheduled from the following day up to 90 days in the future

Payoneer provides mass payout solutions for marketplaces, enabling payments to thousands of vendors across multiple countries (Payoneer, n.d.).

Citation: Payoneer FAQ: Scheduling Single and Recurring Payments (n.d.); Payoneer: Payout Solution for Marketplaces (n.d.).

4a. Supplementary Research: Additional Marketplace Payment Hold Policies

This section extends the industry benchmark analysis to eight additional marketplace platforms: TodayTix, ClassPass, Too Good To Go, Gametime, Lyft, Uber, Groupon, and LivingSocial. These platforms were selected to capture a broader cross-section of marketplace types—ticketing, wellness subscriptions, food waste reduction, rideshare, and daily-deal commerce. Payment hold practices are examined using publicly available policies, help documentation, merchant terms, and corroborated third-party reporting.

4a.1 Research Methodology Note

Where official partner-facing payment terms are not publicly disclosed (as is the case for TodayTix), findings draw on industry-wide standards for the relevant vertical (live ticketing), disclosed buyer-facing terms, and corroborated third-party sources. All findings are cited inline and in the References section.

4a.2 TodayTix

Hold Period: Not publicly disclosed; estimated 7–30 days post-event, consistent with live entertainment industry norms
Payout Trigger: After ticket redemption / event completion
Payment Method: Not disclosed publicly

TodayTix operates as an authorized ticketing agent and reseller for theatres, Broadway productions, and cultural venues globally (TodayTix, n.d.-a). The financial arrangements between TodayTix and its theatre or venue partners—including specific payment hold periods or payout schedules—are governed by private contractual agreements and are not disclosed in publicly available documentation (TodayTix, n.d.-b).

Drawing on the broader live entertainment industry, comparable ticketing intermediaries such as Ticketmaster settle with venues approximately 7 days after the event date, while UK Theatre's contractual guidelines recommend final settlement within 20–30 working days following the closing performance (UK Theatre, n.d.; Ticketmaster, n.d.). Broadway Play Publishing Inc. requires payment within three business days of a production's closing date (Broadway Play Publishing Inc., n.d.). Given TodayTix's positioning as a premium mobile ticketing platform operating across both commercial and institutional theatrical markets, a reasonable inference is that partner payments are released post-event, likely within 7–30 calendar days, aligned with standard box office settlement windows.

Key Risk Factor: Because TodayTix sells last-minute and rush tickets for same-day or near-term performances, the effective hold window from ticket sale to payout is naturally compressed compared to advance-sale platforms.

Citation: TodayTix Terms of Use (n.d.-a); TodayTix Partner Program Overview (n.d.-b); UK Theatre Ticketing & Settlement Guidelines (n.d.); Ticketmaster Payment Policy (n.d.); Broadway Play Publishing (n.d.).

4a.3 ClassPass

Hold Period: Approximately 15–45 days (funds held for prior month's reservations, paid by the 15th of the following month)
Payout Schedule: Monthly (most partners); bi-monthly for NYC-based studios; bi-weekly for Mindbody-integrated partners
Payment Processor: Tipalti (third-party)

ClassPass pays its partner studios through Tipalti, a secure third-party payment platform (Mindbody, n.d.). The standard payout schedule releases funds for all reservations completed in the prior calendar month by the 15th of the following month (ClassPass Help, n.d.). This means most studios wait up to approximately 45 days from the date of the first class in a calendar month before receiving payment for that month's bookings—a material hold period relative to real-time settlement.

New York City-based partners are subject to a bi-monthly schedule: reservations from the 1st–15th of the month are paid by the 30th of the same month; reservations from the 16th–30th are paid by the 15th of the following month. Studios using Mindbody as their booking system and integrated with ClassPass may qualify for bi-weekly payouts (ClassPass Help, n.d.).

Additional Terms: ClassPass retains all late cancellation and no-show fees charged to users; studios are not entitled to these amounts. ClassPass pays for spots occupied by its users regardless of late cancellation in most circumstances. Studios do not receive compensation for classes attended under a ClassPass free trial promotion (ClassPass Help, n.d.).

Citation: ClassPass Studio Partner Help Center (n.d.); Mindbody ClassPass Integration Documentation (n.d.).

4a.4 Too Good To Go

Hold Period: Approximately 60–90+ days (quarterly cycle, paid within 7–10 working days of the new quarter)
Payout Schedule: Quarterly (standard); monthly for long-standing partners
Commission: Fixed fee or 25% + VAT of the Surprise Bag price

Too Good To Go compensates its store partners on a quarterly basis, with payouts processed within the first 7–10 working days of each new payment cycle (ReLondon, n.d.). This means restaurants, bakeries, and food retailers may wait an entire fiscal quarter—up to 90 or more days—before receiving payment for Surprise Bags already sold to consumers. Long-standing partners may be eligible for a monthly payout schedule (Reddit, n.d.-a).

Partners must also accumulate a minimum number of Surprise Bag sales before receiving an initial payout, which further extends the effective hold period for newly onboarded vendors (Reddit, n.d.-b). Too Good To Go charges an annual administration fee (£39 + VAT in the UK) and deducts either a fixed per-bag fee or 25% + VAT of the bag's consumer price from remittances (ReLondon, n.d.).

Comparative Note: Too Good To Go's quarterly payment model is one of the longest standard hold periods identified across all platforms in this report. The extended cycle appears to reflect the platform's mission-oriented, cost-reduction model rather than risk management, as orders are pre-paid by consumers and food is already prepared.

Citation: ReLondon Too Good To Go Partner Guide (n.d.); Reddit r/TooGoodToGo community threads on payment schedules (n.d.-a, n.d.-b).

4a.5 Gametime

Hold Period: Funds held until 48 hours after the event concludes
Payout Schedule: 48 hours post-event
Payment Method: PayPal exclusively
Minimum Payout Threshold: $25 per calendar month

Gametime United, which operates as the payment agent for ticket resellers on its platform, releases seller payments 48 hours after the event has taken place, provided that tickets were successfully delivered to the buyer (Gametime, n.d.-a). All payouts are disbursed exclusively via PayPal, requiring sellers to maintain a valid PayPal email address on their account (Gametime, n.d.-b).

Gametime's payment policy functions as an event-contingent escrow: funds from ticket sales are held in full until after the event concludes and delivery is confirmed, at which point the 48-hour window begins. Sellers receive a text notification once their tickets are sold; the payout follows post-event (Gametime, n.d.-c).

Minimum Threshold: Gametime will remit payments when the cumulative amount due reaches at least $25 within a calendar month. If proceeds fall below $25 in a given month, the balance accrues and is included in the next month's payment when the threshold is met (Gametime, n.d.-d).

Comparative Note: Gametime's post-event 48-hour hold is consistent with established secondary ticketing market practice and reflects the platform's need to confirm attendance-based delivery before releasing funds—a model analogous to Airbnb's post-check-in release policy.

Citation: Gametime Seller FAQ (n.d.-a); Gametime Sell Your Tickets Page (n.d.-b); Gametime How It Works (n.d.-c); Gametime United Seller Payment Terms (n.d.-d).

4a.6 Lyft

Hold Period: No explicit hold period; funds accumulate over a weekly cycle
Payout Schedule: Weekly (Monday 5:00 AM to Monday 4:59 AM cycle; transfers initiated Tuesday; deposited Wednesday–Friday)
Instant Options: Express Pay (debit card, $1.25–$1.75 fee); Lyft Direct (no fee)

Lyft pays drivers on a weekly schedule with no explicitly stated payment hold period (Lyft Help, n.d.-a). The earnings cycle runs Monday 5:00 AM to the following Monday 4:59 AM; accumulated earnings are transferred from the driver's Lyft account to their linked bank account every Tuesday. Depending on the driver's bank, deposits typically arrive Wednesday through Friday of the same week (Lyft Help, n.d.-b).

Express Pay allows drivers to cash out instantly to a linked debit card for a fee of $1.25 (increasing to $1.75 as of February 25, 2026). Most Express Pay transfers complete within 24 hours. A minimum of $6.25 in available earnings is required ($5 minimum in New York, with a once-per-day limit). New card additions or changes carry a 48-hour activation delay before Express Pay becomes available (Lyft Help, n.d.-c).

Lyft Direct provides drivers with an integrated business debit card and bank account, enabling instant, fee-free payouts after every ride completion (Lyft Help, n.d.-d).

Lyft also guarantees that a driver's weekly earnings will equal at least 70% of passenger payments after external fees, with the platform covering any shortfall (Lyft, n.d.-e).

Citation: Lyft Help: When you get paid (n.d.-a); Lyft Help: Driver earnings and payment schedule (n.d.-b); Lyft Help: Express Pay (n.d.-c); Lyft Help: Lyft Direct (n.d.-d); Lyft Driver Earnings Guarantee (n.d.-e).

4a.7 Uber (Updated Detail)

Hold Period: No explicit hold period; weekly earnings cycle
Payout Schedule: Weekly (cycle starts Monday 4:00 AM; direct deposit initiated Monday/Tuesday; arrives 1–5 business days later)
Instant Options: Instant Pay / Cash Out (up to 5–6x/day, ~$0.50–$1.25 fee); Uber Pro Card (instant, fee-free)

Uber drivers are paid on a weekly earnings cycle, running Monday 4:00 AM to the following Monday 3:59 AM local time, with direct deposit initiated at the start of the following week (Uber Help, n.d.-a). Once processed, the transfer takes 1–5 business days to appear in a driver's bank account, depending on the financial institution's processing times (Uber Help, n.d.-b). Banking holidays may further delay deposits.

Uber does not impose a deliberate payment hold. The delay between earning and receipt is attributable to standard ACH bank processing windows rather than a platform-imposed reserve (Uber Help, n.d.-c).

Instant Pay (Cash Out) allows drivers to access available earnings up to 5–6 times per day for a fee (typically $0.50–$1.25; free in New York City). While most Instant Pay transfers are immediate, some banks may take 2–3 days to process them. New drivers must typically complete 25+ trips and have been active for 14 days to gain Instant Pay eligibility (Uber Help, n.d.-d).

Uber Pro Card is a business debit card offering automatic, fee-free payouts after each completed trip, plus cash-back benefits on fuel and EV charging (Uber Help, n.d.-e).

Citation: Uber Help: Understanding your earnings timeline (n.d.-a); Uber Help: How payments work (n.d.-b); Uber Help: Direct deposit (n.d.-c); Uber Help: Instant Pay / Cash Out (n.d.-d); Uber Help: Uber Pro Card (n.d.-e).

4a.8 Groupon

Hold Period: Redemption-contingent; payments processed weekly (Wednesdays)
Payout Schedule: Weekly on Wednesdays (excluding last 5 business days of month); ACH arrival 3–5 business days after processing
Hold-Back: 20% of Net Remittance Amount held until promotional voucher value expiration date

Groupon remits payments to merchants for redeemed vouchers only—vendors receive no payment for sold-but-unredeemed vouchers until redemption occurs (Groupon Merchant Help, n.d.-a). Payment runs occur once per week, typically on Wednesdays. An important constraint: Groupon does not process payments during the last five business days of any calendar month; invoices generated during that window are held and paid on the first business day of the following month (Groupon Merchant Help, n.d.-b).

After Groupon processes a payment, ACH transactions generally take an additional 3–5 business days to appear in connected merchant bank accounts (Groupon Merchant Help, n.d.-a). Weekends and US bank holidays further delay processing.

20% Holdback: Groupon withholds 20% of the Net Remittance Amount from merchant payouts as a reserve against potential refunds and chargebacks. This reserve is released—and included in a subsequent payment—after the promotional value expiration date of the associated vouchers has passed (Groupon Merchant Agreement, n.d.-c). Combined with the redemption-only trigger, Groupon's effective hold window can be substantial: a merchant may wait weeks or months for full settlement if customers delay redemption.

Merchant Tracking: Merchants can monitor scheduled and historical payments through the "Payments" tab in the Groupon Merchant Center dashboard (Groupon Merchant Help, n.d.-b).

Citation: Groupon Merchant Center Help: When do I get paid? (n.d.-a); Groupon Merchant Center: Payment Schedule Details (n.d.-b); Groupon Merchant Agreement and Terms (n.d.-c).

4a.9 LivingSocial

Hold Period: 80% paid within 10 business days per 14-day window; 20% held until promotional value expiration
Payout Schedule: Staged remittance per 14-calendar-day deal windows; weekly payment runs (Wednesday submission → 10 business days)
Relationship: Now operated under Groupon

LivingSocial—now operated under Groupon's corporate umbrella—applies a staged payment model tied to active deal windows of 14 calendar days each (LivingSocial Merchant Agreement, n.d.). For vouchers sold during the initial 14-day promotion window (the "Initial Feature Date"), LivingSocial remits 80% of the Net Remittance Amount (gross sales minus applicable fees, returns, and refunds) within ten business days after that window closes.

For vouchers sold during each subsequent 14-day window ("Additional Feature Dates"), an additional 80% remittance of the net earned during that window is paid within ten business days after each window closes. Simultaneously, any previously held 20% reserve from prior windows is also released and included in these ongoing payments—but only after the promotional value expiration date for those vouchers has passed (LivingSocial Merchant Agreement, n.d.).

In practice, LivingSocial conducts weekly payment runs. Merchants who submit redemption confirmations and required documentation (e.g., proof of postage or booking confirmation) by 2:00 PM on a Wednesday can expect payment within approximately 10 business days, subject to bank holiday calendars. International bank transfers may take longer (LivingSocial UK Help, n.d.).

Comparative Note: LivingSocial's two-part payout structure—80% up front (within 10 business days of each 14-day window) and 20% deferred until voucher expiry—is among the most complex payment architectures in this study. The deferred 20% component functions as a merchant-held reserve for consumer refund risk and can remain outstanding for the full duration of the voucher's validity period, which may extend months beyond the original deal date.

Citation: LivingSocial Merchant Agreement (n.d.); LivingSocial UK Help: When will I receive payment? (n.d.); Groupon/LivingSocial Merchant Terms (via Groupon.com).

5. Payment Processor Guidance: Stripe Connect

5.1 Manual Payouts and Maximum Hold Periods

Stripe Connect supports manual payouts to hold funds in connected accounts' balances before disbursement. The maximum holding period depends on the business's country (Stripe, n.d.-a):

CountryMaximum Holding Period
United States2 years
Thailand10 days
All other countries90 days

Critical Distinction: Stripe clarifies that it does not provide true escrow services or escrow accounts. Manual payouts simply allow platforms to control payout timing within these maximum periods.

Citation: Stripe Documentation: Using Manual Payouts (n.d.-a).

5.2 Payment Authorization Holds

Stripe allows placing holds on payment methods by separating authorization from capture (Stripe, n.d.-b). Authorization validity windows: Card-not-present: Visa (5-7 days), Mastercard/AmEx/Discover (7 days); Card-present: Visa (5 days), Mastercard/AmEx/Discover (2 days); Japan: JPY up to 30 days.

Citation: Stripe Documentation: Place a Hold on a Payment Method (n.d.-b).

5.3 Marketplace Payment Architecture

Stripe recommends using separate charges and transfers or destination charges for marketplaces (Stripe, n.d.-c). The platform is the merchant of record; the platform's account balance covers Stripe fees, refunds, and chargebacks.

Citation: Stripe Documentation: Build a Marketplace (n.d.-c).

5.4 Dispute and Chargeback Management

Stripe emphasizes that platforms are ultimately liable for chargebacks and monitoring program fines (Stripe, n.d.-d). Key practices: balance checks before refunds, vendor education on disputes, meeting evidence deadlines, promptly reversing transfers after chargebacks.

Citation: Stripe Documentation: Handle Refunds and Disputes (n.d.-d).

6. Business Analysis: Strategic Considerations

6.1 Benefits of Payment Holds

Fraud and Chargeback Protection: Holding payments reduces fraud exposure and chargeback risk (LockTrust, n.d.). High chargeback ratios can result in merchant account termination, increased interchange fees, and monitoring program fines.

Cash Flow Management: Provides working capital buffer for refunds, platform fees, seasonal fluctuations, and reserves.

Dispute Resolution Leverage: Funds held provide leverage for dispute resolution and quality standards.

Compliance and Regulatory Protection: Allows time for fraud reviews, KYC/AML verification, and compliance checks.

6.2 Costs and Risks of Payment Holds

Seller Retention and Churn: Faster payouts significantly improve seller retention—payment speed beats take rate in seller economics (Torsion, n.d.). Instant settlement can reduce seller churn by 15-40%. On a $5M monthly GMV marketplace, delayed payouts can cost sellers $93K+ annually in financing costs.

Competitive Disadvantage: Marketplaces with faster payout cycles gain competitive advantage in vendor acquisition and retention.

Working Capital Burden: Traditional 7-30 day settlement cycles create significant financing burden for sellers.

Operational Complexity: Requires reconciliation systems, dispute management infrastructure, compliance monitoring (AppScrip, n.d.).

Regulatory and Custody Risk: Holding funds creates regulatory burden and custody liability (TrustlessWork, n.d.).

Payment Processing Cost Stack: Card processing (2.2-3%), gateway fees (0.2-0.6%), vendor payouts ($0.30-$1.50 each), chargebacks ($15-25 each), compliance costs (0.2-0.8%). A 12% commission may yield only 4-6% true platform revenue (CS-Cart, n.d.).

7. Policy Options and Trade-offs

Six policy options are analyzed: (1) No Hold Period—maximum retention, highest risk; (2) Short Hold (1-3 days)—balance of satisfaction and protection; (3) Standard Hold (7-14 days)—industry standard, Amazon-aligned; (4) Variable Hold (risk-based)—optimizes for both objectives; (5) Milestone-Based Escrow—strongest protection, highest complexity; (6) Hybrid Approach—combines strategies for mature platforms.

Recommended: Option 4 (Variable Hold Period) or Option 6 (Hybrid) for platforms with sufficient data and infrastructure. Option 3 (Standard 7-day hold) for new or smaller platforms.

8. Recommendations

8.1 Recommended Policy Framework

  • Standard Hold: 7 days after confirmed service completion
  • New Vendors: Up to 14 days for first 5-10 successful transactions
  • Trusted Vendors: 1-3 days for vendors with 50+ transactions, <2% refund rate, >4.5 rating
  • High-Risk: 14-21 days or milestone-based for flagged transactions

8.2 Implementation Considerations

Use Stripe Connect with manual payouts; clearly disclose hold periods; implement fraud detection and vendor performance tracking; build automated payout scheduling and dispute management.

8.3 Key Metrics to Monitor

Seller churn rate, chargeback rate, refund rate, payment processing costs, vendor satisfaction, cash flow, compliance metrics.

9. Conclusion

Legal Summary: No explicit federal limit; FTC Section 5 requires disclosure and non-unfair practices; Regulation E governs consumer payments; unclaimed property laws create 3-5 year outer limits; Stripe allows up to 2 years in US.

Industry Summary: Major marketplaces converge on 0-7 day hold periods, with longer holds for new/high-risk vendors only.

Business Summary: Faster payouts improve retention (15-40% churn reduction); longer holds reduce fraud but increase churn.

Final Recommendations: Start with 7-day standard hold; implement risk-based tiers; prioritize faster payouts for trusted vendors; ensure clear disclosure; monitor metrics; consult qualified counsel.
